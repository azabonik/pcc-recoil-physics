<Section heading="Reading the three primary outputs">
  <p>
    The calculator returns seven numbers. Three carry most of the practical
    meaning: Power Factor and the bullet/gas split describe what's coming out
    the muzzle, but it's the three below that describe what's coming out the
    back, and how it feels.
  </p>

  <FactorGrid>
    <FactorCard accent="red" title="Total recoil momentum" verdict="Set by the ammo. Tune the gas half.">
      The full backward impulse the gun must absorb, in gr·fps. Bullet
      momentum is locked by Power Factor; gas momentum is the only piece a
      handloader directly controls. Independent of gun weight: a heavier
      gun absorbs the same impulse, just at a lower speed.
    </FactorCard>
    <FactorCard accent="amber" title="Recoil velocity" verdict="Set by the platform. Heavier gun, slower gun.">
      How fast the gun moves backward, in fps. Total momentum divided by
      gun mass: same ammo, half the gun weight, double the gun speed.
      V<sub>gun</sub> drives the impulse your hand redirects and how
      quickly the sights return for the follow-up.
    </FactorCard>
    <FactorCard accent="blue" title="Free recoil energy" verdict="The compound result. Mass × tuning.">
      The kinetic energy of the gun moving backward, in ft·lb (½ m V<sub>gun</sub>²).
      The single most useful comparison metric: it folds speed and mass
      into one number for what your grip, wrists, and shoulders dissipate
      per shot.
    </FactorCard>
  </FactorGrid>

  <h3>How to lower total recoil momentum</h3>

  <p>
    Bullet momentum is the floor: whatever the rules require, that's the
    bottom of the chart. Below it, gas momentum is the part you have real
    leverage over. For typical 9mm loads it's 3% to 8% of total momentum;
    for a comped Major load it can exceed 20%.
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li>
      <strong>Run the lightest charge that meets PF.</strong> Faster powders
      develop the same peak pressure with less mass, so the gas half of the
      equation shrinks.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Lower the gas factor.</strong> SAAMI publishes a single
      f = 1.50 for "Pistol &amp; revolvers" with no breakdown by powder
      type or barrel length (2018 SAAMI Free Recoil Energy technical
      document, derived from the British Textbook of Small Arms 1929 range
      of 1 to 2). Fast powder in a longer barrel pulls f toward the lower
      bound of that range because gas has more expansion length and less
      residual pressure at the muzzle; slow powder in a short barrel
      pulls it toward the upper bound. The specific sub-1.50 anchors you'll
      see quoted on this site (≈ 1.0 for fully expanded fast powder in a
      long barrel) are analytical estimates bounded by the British range,
      not measurements. 9mm doesn't reach SAAMI's rifle anchor of 1.75
      because rifle-burn-rate powders aren't loaded in 9mm cases.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Heavier bullet at lower velocity, same PF.</strong> Bullet
      momentum doesn't change. But the slower acceleration usually needs
      less powder and gives the gas more time to expand, so the gas half
      shrinks.
    </li>
  </ul>

  <h3>How to lower recoil velocity</h3>

  <p>
    V<sub>gun</sub> is where gun mass enters the math. It scales linearly
    with mass: doubling the gun weight halves V<sub>gun</sub> for the same
    ammo.
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li>
      <strong>Heavier gun.</strong> A 7.5 lb PCC firing the same ammo as a
      2.5 lb subcompact moves backward at one-third the speed. This is most
      of why PCCs feel near-recoilless even with full-power loads.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Add muzzle weight on a pistol.</strong> Tungsten guide rods,
      heavier slides, comp-plus-weight setups. They don't change PF or the
      ammo, but every ounce added lowers V<sub>gun</sub> in proportion to
      the new total mass.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Reduce total momentum.</strong> The handload levers above
      show up linearly here: a 5% drop in total momentum is a 5% drop in
      V<sub>gun</sub>.
    </li>
  </ul>

  <h3>How to lower free recoil energy</h3>

  <p>
    The squared relationship in ½ m V<sub>gun</sub>² is the most important
    fact about this output. It cuts two ways depending on which lever
    you're pulling:
  </p>

  <ul>
    <li>
      <strong>Ammo-side reductions compound.</strong> A drop in total
      momentum appears linearly in V<sub>gun</sub>, then gets squared into
      energy. A 5% reduction in total momentum from charge and gas-factor
      tuning yields roughly a 10% reduction in free recoil energy.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Gun-side reductions are linear, but the multiplier is large.</strong>
      Doubling gun mass halves V<sub>gun</sub>, and the squared term in the
      energy formula cancels against the leading mass, so energy also halves.
      Tripling gun mass cuts energy to one-third.
    </li>
  </ul>

  <p>
    The two stack. A tuned load in a heavy gun is the lowest energy figure
    you can reach without dropping below the PF floor. Gun mass is set once
    when you pick the platform; it's the single biggest lever. Charge and
    powder choice is the per-load tuning that wrings the last 10% to 20%
    out of whatever platform you're shooting.
  </p>

  <Callout title="Same load, three guns">
    <p>
      124 gr at 1050 fps, 4.0 gr charge, gas factor 1.5 (a typical 130 PF
      Minor load):
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li>
        <strong>2.5 lb subcompact:</strong> V<sub>gun</sub> ≈ 7.8 fps,
        energy ≈ 2.36 ft·lb.
      </li>
      <li>
        <strong>3.0 lb full-size pistol:</strong> V<sub>gun</sub> ≈ 6.5 fps,
        energy ≈ 1.97 ft·lb. 17% less energy for 0.5 lb more gun.
      </li>
      <li>
        <strong>7.5 lb PCC:</strong> V<sub>gun</sub> ≈ 2.6 fps,
        energy ≈ 0.79 ft·lb. 66% less energy than the subcompact, same ammo.
      </li>
    </ul>
    <p>
      The bullet did the same work in every case. The gun absorbed it very
      differently.
    </p>
  </Callout>
