    SECTION 1 — THE EQUATION

<Section number="01" label="The Recoil Equation" heading="Recoil is more than bullet momentum">
  <p>
    When a round fires, two things fly out the front of the barrel: the bullet, and a blast of hot gas
    from the burned powder. Both push the gun backward. Most shooters only think about the bullet — but
    the gas matters too, especially in a long-barreled PCC.<Sup refs={[1, 2, 3]} />
  </p>

  <PlainEnglish>
    <p>
      In plain terms: recoil = how hard the bullet pushes backward + how hard the gas pushes backward.
      You can't change the bullet push (that's your Power Factor). But you CAN change the gas push
      by choosing a different powder.
    </p>
  </PlainEnglish>

  <EquationBox label="Free Recoil Velocity (SAAMI / Hatcher / NRA)">
    <div class="eq">
      <span class="var">V</span><sub>gun</sub> &nbsp;=&nbsp;
      ( <span class="highlight"><span class="var">m</span><sub>bullet</sub> × <span class="var">V</span><sub>muzzle</sub></span>
      &nbsp;+&nbsp;
      <span class="blue-hl"><span class="var">m</span><sub>charge</sub> × <span class="var">V</span><sub>gas</sub></span> )
      &nbsp;÷&nbsp; <span class="var">M</span><sub>gun</sub>
    </div>
    <div class="eq-note">
      Where <span class="accent"><span class="var">V</span><sub>gas</sub></span><span> = </span><span class="var">f</span><span> × </span><span class="var">V</span><sub>muzzle</sub><br />
      <span>SAAMI gas factor </span><span class="var">f</span><span> ≈ 1.5 for handguns, 1.75 for rifles</span><Sup refs={1} /><br />
      <span>British Textbook of Small Arms (1929): </span><span class="var">f</span><span> "lies between 1 and 2, with average value 1½"</span><Sup refs={3} />
    </div>
  </EquationBox>

  <VariableLegend />

  <p>
    The <span style="color:var(--accent);font-weight:600">orange term</span> (bullet push) is locked once you pick your bullet weight and PF target.
    The <span style="color:var(--blue);font-weight:600">blue term</span> (gas push) is the only variable a handloader can manipulate — and
    the theoretical ideal is to drive it to zero. That happens when gas pressure at the muzzle
    equals atmospheric pressure: no pressure differential, no gas acceleration, no blast, no kick
    from gas. In practice you can't reach zero, but every psi above atmospheric is wasted energy
    that becomes recoil, blast, and noise instead of bullet velocity.
  </p>
</Section>

{/* ============================================================================
    SECTION 2 — THE GOAL
    ============================================================================ */}
<Section number="02" label="The Goal: Atmospheric Pressure at the Muzzle" heading="Every psi above atmospheric is wasted as recoil">
  <p>
    If gas pressure inside the barrel dropped to 14.7 psi (atmospheric) right as the bullet exits,
    there would be no pressure differential between bore and open air. Gas wouldn't accelerate out the muzzle.
    V<sub>gas</sub> would approach zero. No blast. No gas recoil. Total recoil would equal pure bullet momentum —
    nothing else. That's the theoretical floor.
  </p>
  <p>
    You can't actually reach it — the bullet needs some residual gas pressure to overcome bore friction,
    and hot gas at atmospheric pressure still pops when it hits cooler ambient air. But the closer you
    get, the less recoil, blast, and noise you produce. Two things move you toward atmospheric:
    a powder that finishes burning early (so gas has room to expand) and a barrel long enough to let
    that expansion happen.<Sup refs={[4, 5]} />
  </p>

  <PlainEnglish>
    <p>
      The barrel after the powder finishes burning is just an expansion chamber. The longer the gas
      has to expand, the lower its pressure when it finally exits. Fast powder in a long barrel
      gives the gas the most room to decompress. Slow powder in the same barrel is still generating
      pressure right up to the muzzle — the gas never gets a chance to settle.
    </p>
  </PlainEnglish>

  <BarrelDiagram />
</Section>

{/* ============================================================================
    SECTION 3 — DIMINISHING RETURNS
    ============================================================================ */}
<Section number="03" label="How Much Barrel Do You Need to Reach Atmospheric?" heading="Diminishing returns: you can approach atmospheric but never reach it">
  <p>
    Gas expansion follows roughly P ∝ V<sup style="font-size:10px;">−γ</sup> — each doubling of expansion volume drops
    pressure by about 55–60%, but you can never reach zero (or atmospheric). The first few inches of
    expansion after burn-out produce the biggest pressure drop. After that, each additional inch matters less.
    There's a barrel length where the gas is close enough to atmospheric that more barrel just adds friction.
  </p>

  <PlainEnglish>
    <p>
      Think of it like opening a pressure cooker. Cracking the lid an inch releases a huge blast of steam.
      Opening it another inch releases less. By the time it's wide open, the pressure inside has equalized
      with the room — more opening changes nothing. Barrel length works the same way: you're trying to
      let the gas equalize with the atmosphere before it exits.
    </p>
  </PlainEnglish>

  <BarrelLengthZones />

  <Callout title="What this means for short-barrel PCC builds">
    <p>
      If you're running a 7–8″ AR-9 or MPX-K, you're in the transition zone. Fast powder still wins
      over slow powder (less gas mass is always less gas mass), but the expansion benefit is only partial.
      The blast and sound reduction will be noticeable but not as dramatic as from a 13″+ barrel.
      You're getting maybe <strong>half the perceptual benefit</strong> compared to a full-length PCC.
      If minimum recoil is the goal and you're choosing between barrel lengths, the jump from
      8″ to 10–11″ buys more than the jump from 13″ to 16″.
    </p>
  </Callout>
</Section>

  <MethodNote>
    <strong>A note on the f estimates:</strong>
    SAAMI uses a single f = 1.5 for all handguns regardless of barrel length or burn rate.
    There is no published SAAMI category for PCCs. The f ≈ 1.0 (fast powder, fully expanded)
    and f ≈ 1.8 (slow powder, still pressurized) used above are analytical estimates
    bounded by the British Textbook range of 1–2.<Sup refs={6} />
    If SAAMI's standard f = 1.5 were applied to both loads, the recoil difference would be
    ~3% (driven purely by the charge weight difference: 4.0 gr vs 6.0 gr). The true value
    is likely between 3% and 8%, depending on actual muzzle pressure — which has not been
    measured for 9mm PCC with variable burn rates in any published study.
  </MethodNote>

  <h2 class="subhead">The powder is the primary variable, not the bullet weight</h2>
  <p>
    Across all three weights, the gas momentum roughly triples between fast and slow powder.
    But notice the pattern: at equal PF with the same fast powder, heavier bullets produce
    <em>less</em> gas momentum — because lower muzzle velocity means lower V<sub>gas</sub> and a
    smaller charge. The bullet weight doesn't drive the recoil difference. The powder does.
    Light bullets enter the picture because they're the weight class that lets you run
    ultra-fast powders at charge weights large enough for consistent metering and ignition,
    while maintaining the velocity needed for flat trajectories at PCC distances.
  </p>

{/* ============================================================================
    SECTION 5 — PERCEPTUAL FACTORS
    ============================================================================ */}
<Section number="05" label="The Other 80%: Why It Feels Bigger Than the Math Says" heading="Muzzle blast, sound, and impulse shape">
  <p>
    The momentum equation says the recoil difference is ~3–8%. But shooters say it feels much bigger than that.
    They're not wrong — "felt recoil" isn't just about momentum. Three other factors make a huge difference
    in how a shot <em>feels</em>, and all three get dramatically worse when hot, high-pressure gas exits the muzzle.
  </p>

  <FactorGrid>
    <FactorCard accent="red" title="Muzzle Blast" verdict="Probably the single biggest factor in perceived difference">
      High-pressure gas hitting open air creates a shockwave — a sharp
      "punch" you feel on your face, hands, and chest. This isn't recoil
      in the physics sense, but your body registers it as part of the kick.
      Fast powder = low gas pressure at the muzzle = almost no blast.
    </FactorCard>
    <FactorCard accent="amber" title="Sound Level" verdict="Your brain perceives louder as harder-kicking">
      Slow powder exiting a PCC barrel is LOUD — 5–10 dB louder than
      fast powder, which sounds roughly twice as loud to human ears.
      Our brains don't cleanly separate "loud" from "hard-kicking" —
      a louder shot feels like it kicks harder, even if the momentum
      is nearly the same.
    </FactorCard>
    <FactorCard accent="blue" title="Kick Shape" verdict="Same total energy, spread over longer time = softer feel">
      Gas exits after the bullet, delivering a secondary push. With slow powder,
      that push is sharp and sudden — like a slap. With fast powder, the gas
      trickles out gently — like a nudge. Same total push, but the peak force
      is lower, so it feels softer.
    </FactorCard>
  </FactorGrid>

  <Callout title="The Complete Picture">
    <p>
      Fast powder in a PCC produces <strong>~3–8% less recoil momentum</strong> plus a
      <strong> major reduction</strong> in muzzle blast, sound pressure, and secondary impulse sharpness.
      The combination produces a subjective experience that feels like a 20–30% improvement.
      The momentum reduction is real but modest; the perceptual factors are where most of the
      "softness" lives.
    </p>
  </Callout>
</Section>

    SECTION 6 — THE OPTIMIZATION HIERARCHY
    ============================================================================ */}
<Section number="06" label="The Optimization Hierarchy" heading="Minimize muzzle gas pressure. Everything else follows.">
  <p>
    The single principle behind PCC recoil optimization: get the gas as close to atmospheric
    pressure as possible by the time the bullet exits. The three variables that control this
    are, in order of importance:
  </p>

  <table class="calc-table" style="margin-top:24px;">
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Priority</th>
        <th>Variable</th>
        <th>Why It Matters</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td style="font-family:var(--font-mono);font-weight:700;color:var(--accent);font-size:18px;">1</td>
        <td style="font-weight:600;color:var(--text-bright)">Powder burn rate</td>
        <td>The faster the powder finishes burning, the more barrel is available for gas expansion toward atmospheric. This is the dominant variable — it determines how far above atmospheric the gas sits when it exits.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td style="font-family:var(--font-mono);font-weight:700;color:var(--accent);font-size:18px;">2</td>
        <td style="font-weight:600;color:var(--text-bright)">Barrel length</td>
        <td>More barrel after burn completion = more expansion volume = lower muzzle pressure. But diminishing returns: the drop from 8″ to 13″ matters more than 13″ to 18″. There's a barrel length for each powder where you've captured most of the available expansion.</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td style="font-family:var(--font-mono);font-weight:700;color:var(--accent);font-size:18px;">3</td>
        <td style="font-weight:600;color:var(--text-bright)">Bullet weight</td>
        <td>Secondary to powder choice. At equal PF and equal powder, heavier bullets produce <em>less</em> gas momentum (lower velocity = lower V<sub>gas</sub> and smaller charge). But heavier bullets at lower velocity introduce external ballistic penalties — more drop, more lead on movers, longer time-to-target — which matter in PCC stages with 50+ yard engagements. Modern roller-delayed and tunable-gas PCCs (JP5, Mean Arms, etc.) mitigate the mechanical cycling concerns that older blowback designs impose on charge weight. Choose the weight that balances gas optimization against the ballistic demands of your match stages.</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

  <p style="margin-top:24px;">
    In a pistol (4–5″ barrel), this hierarchy barely applies. The barrel is too short for meaningful gas
    expansion regardless of powder choice — muzzle pressure is high with any load. Pistol recoil is
    dominated by slide dynamics, where heavy bullets produce a softer impulse. PCC recoil is dominated
    by gas state at the muzzle, where powder selection and barrel length determine how close you get
    to atmospheric.
  </p>

    <p>
      Within the optimized section, the spread across all bullet weights is ~2.7%. Across the
      two sections, the gap is ~18–20%. Powder selection for the barrel matters roughly
      7× more than bullet weight. The gas momentum column tells you why: optimized loads keep
      gas under 6,200; pistol loads let it run up to 8,100 — and that's just the momentum.
      The blast, sound, and impulse-shape differences between near-atmospheric gas and
      still-pressurized gas are on top of that.
    </p>

  <MethodNote>
    <strong>For other Power Factors:</strong> multiply velocity by (target PF ÷ 130).
    Example: 125 PF with 95 gr → 1,368 × (125 ÷ 130) = 1,316 fps.
    Gas momentum scales proportionally — the relative differences between weights remain roughly the same.
  </MethodNote>

  <SourceItem num={6}>
    The gas velocity factors used in this reference (f ≈ 1.0 for fast powder in a 13″ PCC barrel, f ≈ 1.4 for medium powder, f ≈ 1.8 for slow powder) are analytical estimates, not measurements. They are bounded by the British Textbook range [1, 2] and informed by FIRGELLI's documented range of 1.2–1.75.<Sup refs={[3, 4]} /> The standard SAAMI method uses f = 1.5 for all handguns regardless of burn rate or barrel length; SAAMI does not publish a PCC-specific factor. If f = 1.5 is applied uniformly, the momentum difference between fast and slow powder at equal PF reduces to ~3% (driven by charge weight alone). The variable-f approach is more physically defensible for PCC barrel lengths but has not been validated by controlled measurement. Charge weight estimates throughout are interpolated from published load data and should be treated as approximations. Perceptual factors (blast, sound, impulse shape) are qualitative observations consistent with muzzle pressure physics but not quantified by controlled study for 9mm PCC specifically.
